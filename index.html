One language, a thousand ports.

Your family may have left southern Fujian generations ago — for Manila, Penang, Medan, Singapore, Cebu, or ports even farther away. But the words never left. They remain in the food on your table, the names your grandparents used, the gods beside the family altar, and the stories nobody thought to write down. Welcome to the worldwide home of Hokkien heritage — our roots, our tongue, our faith, our food, and the letters that once crossed the sea.

You may already speak more Hokkien
than you think.

You just never knew where the words came from. If you've ever ordered any of these — you were speaking the language of Quanzhou and Zhangzhou, carried across generations, often without a single formal lesson. Tens of millions of people around the world trace their families to the Hokkien ports of southern Fujian. Most of us can't read the characters anymore. This site is for us.

批

LETTERS — How they stayed connected.

Qiaopi 僑批 — a letter and a remittance in one envelope, carried by couriers across the South China Sea for over a century. Money for the family, a few lines of news, a name signed in characters the next generation would forget how to read. We are collecting these stories — including yours.
